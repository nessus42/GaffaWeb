The story how I did come across this info is a bit interesting:

On my recent trip to Australia I was going with a group in a bus. One night
we were in a disco, and I said that this wasn't really my music (the music
the disco played). Now to make me more happy, one of the girls in our group
asked the VJ (not a DJ, Australian discos most often play videos instead of
records) about whether he could help my taste. Well, he couldn't (or else
the disco would've been quite empty quite fast, something which I already
told the girl inquiring for me), but on returning to me the girl said the
VJ had several Kate videos.

Well, that did cost me an hour with a bent back writing notes down on a piece
of paper standing next to the VJ while the VJ played the usual disco stuff.
This hour brought me the first three video tapes (those without total
playing time), three 24-track-tapes. Finding them amongst the 100+
tapes he got was easy, since the whole stock is in a computer database
which also controls the two VCRs - you select the tracks, the computer asks
for the tapes and fast forwards, plays, rewinds and ejects them automatically.

Two nights later we were in another bar which had the same NightLife video
system running, but with far less tapes. Or so it seemed. At least there
were about 2 dozen tapes lying next to the computer. But this time the
computer found 6 more and one or two of the other ones - the main stock
of video tapes was not lying next to the computer... Anyhow, with 6 more
tapes (now some had 40+ titles) I didn't want to sit there and write down
all the stuff (typing is so much faster), so I took my video camera and
just filmed the tape's back covers.

Several people did ask me what I was doing...

I also found out that it costs the bar Aus-$250 per week to rent the
whole system, tapes included.
