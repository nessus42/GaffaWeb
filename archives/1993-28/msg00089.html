Introduction to the Song of Solomon  (LONG)
*******************************************

[Reference to context of original posting removed :SCTM]

                                 ...following this long introduction
is the complete book from the Bible, "Song of Solomon" (or "Song of
Songs").  I'm sure that most will agree that the Song is a highly
romantic and erotic poem, and certainly sheds some light on the roles
of romantic love and sexuality during the time of King Solomon in
ancient Israel.  I chose the New International Version (NIV)
translation since it is easy to read and that it conveys very well the
erotic nature of the original writing.  There are other good
translations of the Song also, so let's not get into a flame war
regarding which is a more faithful translation, reads better, uses a
better source, etc., although rational and calm comparisons between
translations would be very interesting.  

[Note: I am excerpting the Song without prior permission from the New
York International Bible Society [copyright 1978], but I believe it is
within the commonly accepted guidelines (though pushing the limits I
admit) for use of copyrighted material for scholarly research and
annotation, so I have no problem providing the complete excerpt,
especially since it is needed to support several points I make in the
Introduction.  If you have any problems with my use of this book this
way, feel free to write to the NYIBS and inform them of my grievous
'sin'.  :^) ]  

[You can inform on me too if you like ;-) :SCTM]

It is curious to note that there is a complete lack of any type of
religious content or teaching in the Song.  Unless you knew beforehand
that it is an Old Testament book, you'd never know by its content that
it is considered canon by the Jewish and Christian religions (and
probably also considered divinely inspired by the Moslem faithful -
any feedback here?). 

I also encourage you to either purchase or look at a copy of the NIV
Study Guide Version of Bible (or some other study guide or commentary)
which gives the interested reader a lot more information, verse by
verse, of what is known about the more subtle meanings of the words
and phrases used that were perfectly obvious to a reader in the first
Millennium B.C., but may be obscure or even unknown today. 

I will give the following opinions from a liberal Christian
perspective.  But let's not forget that the modern Jewish and Moslem
religions also regard this book as divinely inspired (at least I'm led
to believe that.)  Thus, I would like to get feedback as to how the
Jewish and Moslem religions view this book. 

I must comment on what I know some conservative and/or traditional
Christians (both Protestant and Catholic) will state in response to
this posting, that is, this book *ultimately* refers to Christ's love
for the Church.  I reject this notion outright for reasons I'll give
below.  I believe the real, but unstated, reason for them *having* to
say this is because the book otherwise would be an embarrassment to
their preconceived notions that sexuality is something that needs to
be repressed and downplayed in proper Christian  practice, even in
marriage. 

I believe the Song of Solomon was written *primarily* with the
spiritual intent of letting people know of the beauty and grandeur of
one of God's greatest gifts to mankind - sexuality.  In fact, I would
say that God's primary purpose for inspiring Solomon (the probable
author) to write the Song of Solomon is for this book to serve as an
ever-present buffer against those misinformed Jewish and Christian
teachers who would arise throughout history and teach, using a
distortion of Scripture, that sexuality is somehow dirty or a
necessary evil for only procreation purposes. 

And despite the presence of the Song of Solomon in the Bible, many
false teachers have been able to effectively divert much mainstream
Christian opinion in the late 19th Century (among other eras) to
accept the incorrect view that human sexuality is "dirty" and a
necessary evil (if you want me to support this contention, I can
e-mail a tract written by a Methodist woman from the late 19th Century
that will make your hair curl with its blatant message that sex is
dirty and a necessary evil to be performed by the wife for the sole
purpose of bearing children.)  I would furthermore assert that
theologians who profess the view that God's ultimate purpose for the
Song of Solomon is to illustrate Christ's love for the Church are
teaching false and, in my opinion, dangerous doctrine, since this can
open the door to the abuses that history shows have occurred.  The
exegesis I've seen from these theologians leaves much to be desired -
about the only arguments that they can make is that for some reason
this book has always been accepted as canon by both Jews and
Christians alike (combined with the Christian view that all Scripture
points to Christ), and that in some places of the New Testament the
relationship of Christ and the Church is talked of as a 'marriage'.
These reasons are too flimsy, in my opinion, to make the quantum leap
to the assertion that the primary purpose of the Song is to illustrate
Christ's love for the Church, beautiful as that comparison is.

However, to use another argument, even if the purpose of the Song of
Solomon were to illustrate Christ's love for the Church, (which should
be clear by now that I reject), the use of erotic allusions and even
explicit sexual language (by our standards as well as the standards of
ancient Israel) automatically adds *legitimacy* to the goodness and
appropriateness (Godliness) of *expressed* and practiced eroticism in
Christian sexuality.  Or putting it another way, God would not have
used such explicit sexual eroticism to describe the relationship of
Christ to the Church unless such sexual eroticism itself is good and
approved in God's eyes. 

Thus, no matter what side of the fence you sit on vis-a-vis the Song's
primary purpose, one can only conclude from this book that eroticism
should play a normal, honored, and yes, talked about (rather than
hidden) role in Christian sexual expression, and that it should be
openly taught to our children in such terms.  As a thought provoking
statement, if Solomon wrote erotic poetry as seen in the Song, then
why don't Christians write erotic literature and poetry today?  

And if you as a Christian are uneasy with what I've said here, then
explore your feelings and go back to Scripture and ask the question to
yourself:  "Why do I feel uneasy?"  I would assert that any uneasiness
is due to the improper teaching of human sexuality that still lingers
on in 20th Century Christian thought and practice.  Of course, I've
heard some argue that sexuality is so "holy" that it is to be kept
private and unspoken, but I believe that that, too, is an incorrect
view with roots in the anti-sexuality Christian teaching from the last
century;  it's the logical response when one is uneasy about talking
about human sexuality. 

According to Tim and Beverly LaHaye, in their book "The Act of
Marriage", (which is a good attempt - by a conservative Christian
couple - to strip away some of the repressive anti-sexuality of the
Christian Church from the 1800's which unfortunately survives to this
day), the Song of Solomon actually describes aspects of sexual
intercourse in a very explicit manner.  For example, in 8:3, 

"His left arm is under my head and his right arm embraces me.",

the word "embraces" actually refers to clitoral stimulation by the man
with the right arm (hand).  It helps explain the anecdote that the
Hebrew bride, when she listened to the words of the Song read at her
wedding, would often blush (this book was apparently read at all
pre-Christian era Jewish marriages).  One question, if your 13 year
old read the book and asked you what this verse means, what would you
say?  If you're uneasy with this question, again explore your feelings
as well as Scripture.  I would have no problem telling my 13 year old
up front exactly what it means since this comes from the Bible.

With respect to the overall eroticism of the Song, 8:10-12 is
illustrative: 

"I belong to my lover, and his desire is for me.  Come, my lover, let
us go to the countryside, let us spend the night in the villages [or
henna bushes].  Let us go early to the vineyards to see if the vines
have budded, if their blossoms have opened, and if the pomegranates
are in bloom - there I will give you my love."

and in 7:6-9,

"How beautiful you are and how pleasing, O love, with your delights!
Your stature is like that of the palm, and your breasts like clusters
of fruit. I said, 'I will climb the palm tree;  I will take hold of
its fruit.'  May your breasts be like the clusters of the vine, the
fragrance of your breath like apples, and your mouth like the best
wine."

Wow!  I daresay that if a Christian writer today wrote something like
this, even using milder and more symbolic language, he/she would be
quickly censored by the so-called Christian "moralists" who would view
this as pornographic.

There are many erotic allusions in the Song that even today we can
still understand (however, some of the symbolism has unfortunately
been lost).  I believe that older teen Christians should be encouraged
to read this book, to help balance out the too many negative messages
(thou shalt nots) that many in Christianity teach today, as well as to
counteract the negative secular messages about sexuality that we are
also inundated with today.  It will give them even something more to
look forward to in marriage and reduce sexual hangups that plague a
large number of Christian and Christian-influenced marriages.

I also believe that Christians should take a more active role in
discussing the beauty and goodness of sexuality rather than to try to
continually hide it or feel ashamed talking about it, or to talk about
it only when referring to what one should NOT do, or that its role is
strictly reproductive, (as I said above, how about Christian erotic
literature?).  This post of the Song of Solomon is an attempt to begin
the process of opening up Christianity to the beauty of human
sexuality - and to begin to talk about it rather than to hide it as if
it doesn't exist. 

End of Introduction

(Note:  There are apparently three voices in the following Biblical erotic
poem, based on the gender of the Hebrew pronouns used in the original text.
They are the woman (the beloved), the man (the lover) and the woman's friends.
It is unclear if the 'king' referred to in the text is the same person as
the man.  Wherever there are difficulties in translation, or notes need to
be given, they are numbered in [..] and explained at the end of the text.)

Woman [1:2-4]   -  Let him kiss me with the kisses of his mouth -

Woman [1:4-7]   -  How right they are to adore you!
                   Dark am I, yet lovely,
                   O daughters of Jerusalem,
                   dark like the tents of Kedar,

                   Why should I be like a veiled woman
                   beside the flocks of your friends?

Man [1:8-11]    -  If you do not know, most beautiful of women,
                   follow the tracks of the sheep

                   Your cheeks are beautiful with earrings,
                   your neck with strings of jewels.
                   We will make you earrings of gold, studded with silver.
