There Goes A Tenner

There Goes A Tenner



When the single for 'The Dreaming' had failed to make any significant impact on the British charts (it hadn't even entered the top 40), another single was needed, and more importantly one that would bring Kate another hit.


OoOoO


In England 'There Goes A Tenner' was chosen as the next release. A rather odd choice, seeing the more catchy and commercial 'Suspended In Gaffa' had been issued in just about every other country. But 'There Goes A Tenner' it was to be.. .

This song had been the quickest of all the 'Dreaming'-tracks to be completed, and it was also one of the few songs to remain contained on one twenty-four track tape instead of two… The majority of the backing tracks were recorded with Nick at The Townhouse, and as all of the other songs of 'The Dreaming', 'Tenner' has to be played loud.


OoOoO


The single was released on Nov 2nd, 1984, and previous to that Kate had already promoted it on two occasions. The first being 'Razzmatazz' on September 21st, where Kate performed the single in the studio, with the help of two male dancers. Then, the second programme was 'Pebble Mill At One'. Kate was interviewed by Paul Gambicni and a snippet of the video got its first (and last) airing on British television...

The video was a very simply but brilliantly done production, telling the song's story quite perfectly… . Especially the swinging of the pendulum is very effective as well as visually appealing. It's a real pity this clip hardly left the archives, and is now hidden away discreetly on the 'The Single File' video.. .


OoOoO


Maybe the great gap between the promotion of the single and the actual release, plus the fact that the very little promotion done had been on a children's tea time show and a lunch time magazine broadcast (hardly peek viewing for the average record-buyer/rock fan) had something to do with the fact that 'There Goes A Tenner' came and went by practically unnoticed.. . EMI also didn't promote it. No displays in the record-shops, no ads in the music press, not a fly-poster to be seen… Most people probably didn't even know the single was out… .


OoOoO


But the reviewers picked up on it though, and here's what they had to say:

"Bush widens her musical scope still further with a practically formless song and no chorus to speak of." - Record Business


"I rather enjoy what is one of the more accessible cuts off 'The Dreaming' album, but hesitate on its commercialness" - Music Week


"A peculiar choice" - N M E


"This has a haunting atmosphere that lingers long after the record has finished" - Smash Hits, reviewed by Neil Tennant of 'Pet Shop Boys' fame


"Too weird to get the airplay she wants" - Melody Maker


"Wait for the next tour" - Sounds


"A bouncy tale of the downfall of Thatcherism. Actually, it's much more trivial than that" - Record Mirror


"Very well planned and executed with relish - Hot Press


OoOoO


It would be a great understatement to say that "There Goes A Tenner" was ignored by radio-stations (it didn't even make the Friday Night review programme) and record-buyers alike. Barely entering the charts at number 93, then sliding down to 95, before vanishing completely… . It was Kate's first real chart flop, and one that had many vicious voices whispering things like 'She's a has-been' and 'She'll never have another hit again'.. How wrong they all turned out to be, when a few years later they were forced to eat their words with the release of 'Running Up That Hill'… .


OoOoO


Finally, here's what Kate had to say about the song, which just for the record, still remains one of my 'The Dreaming'-favourites:

"In that song, the idea is that of everyone's amateur robbers, so it's like maybe they get a bit cocky.. I dunno I've never done a robbery but I think that in a situation like that you'd almost try to be like the person you admire most, so perhaps they'd be like James Cagney and George Raft. That idea was nothing really deep, it was just handy. The real challenge of the song was to make it in a story, but also keep it in a thirties tune"… .
